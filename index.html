Energy basics

The problem we're trying to solve, the vocabulary you need, and how Sweden balances the grid.

How the Swedish grid stays in balance

At any given moment, the energy a country produces must equal the energy it consumes (its load). Put simply: Energy Load = Energy Produced.

The primary responsibility of an electricity provider is to maintain this balance, and to keep the grid running at exactly 50 Hz. That figure is the European standard frequency. Every generator on the continent connected to the grid must spin at 50 Hz at all times.

A typical load reflects what people use electricity for. In a home, this includes appliances, essentials such as lighting and internet, and increasingly an electric or hybrid car charging in the garage. In a factory, the load is far greater, and scales with it's output. Total load therefore varies considerably with population size, the industrial profile of the area, and the time of day, week, and year. It rarely remains same.

Matching production to this moving target is the difficult part. The provider needs a reliable understanding of how consumers use electricity, the ability to correct deviations in real time, and plan for contingency in case of unforeseen events such as weather damage to infrastructure - all while holding the grid at 50 Hz. This matters greatly because the modern grid is tightly interconnected across borders. Sweden sits within the Nordic synchronous area which means it shares the same frequency with Norway, Finland, and eastern Denmark. Without diving into the technical detail, even a small deviation in one country can cause significant disruption for the others connected to it.

In Sweden, electricity comes mostly from hydropower, nuclear and wind, with smaller contributions from biofuels and solar. Most of the hydropower plants are located in the northern and central regions, where the major rivers run, while most of the wind and nuclear capacity is concentrated further south. For an overview of production against load over time, see Energimyndigheten's An energy system in balance; for the geography of individual plants, see Vattenfall's interactive map of power plants; or even this one from grid rebase that has a deatiled map of types of production, amount of it and all the major connections for Sweden Grid Rebase Energy.

Yet most of the population, and most of the industry ie. most of the load is concentrated in the south, close to the ports. This creates the next problem for providers to solve: how to balance consumption concentrated in the south against production concentrated in the north. In practice, roughly 15 to 20 TWh of electricity flows from north to south each year to bridge the gap.

The grid as a highway

A grid simply is the infrastructure that connects consumers (households, businesses and industry) to the energy production sites. The grid functions as an interconnected highway, moving electricity from where it is generated to where it is needed.There is a detailed section later on about how the grid operates.

Most grids are centralised: electricity flows in one direction, from generation to the point of consumption. When providers plan how much electricity to produce, they must account for transmission losses and timing. In practice, this means calculating how much to produce, where it should be delivered, and at what time. Transmission losses are roughly proportional to line length; the longer the line, the larger the loss. This is an important aspect to remember as we introduce sources such as batteries at conusmer level that needs bidirectional flow of electricity to fully utilized

In a long, thin country like Sweden, this north-to-south flow requires long transmission corridors, which in turn carry larger losses for providers to absorb. For the consumer, this translates into higher electricity prices in the south, alongside a longer recovery window in the event of an unforeseen outage.

The Problem Ahead of Us

That is the situation under today's infrastructure. Sweden's annual consumption is projected to rise from around 140 TWh today to 240 to 280 TWh by 2045, roughly double of today's needs. There are three forces drive this growth: previously fossil-dependent industries are to adopting cleaner processes such as electric-arc in steelmaking; the electrification of transport is happening across buses, trucks and cars; and there are major expansion of data centres planned to support AI workloads. These shifts are necessary, and broadly welcome for the overall growth of the society.

The open question is how do we meet the deamdns of the scale-up. Which sources can produce electricity at that volume? Can the existing grid carry the load, and if so on what terms; does it need reinforcement, or should we consider a more decentralised model? What additional services are required to keep it stable? These are the questions the rest of the site will work through.

For a data-driven view of where the demand will materialise geographically, see AI Sweden's behovskartan.se.

This site attempts tp examine how different technologies address these problems for both producers and consumers, introduces the key players,technologies and suppliers, and notes on what is a technological limit and what is an economical limit.

How the grid works

How electricity travels from the power plant to your home, and how the whole system is kept stable.

Before we dive into details, let's get some basics straight about the grid.

As menetioned before, the grid is the electrical highway that carries electricity from where it is generated to our homes and industries. Its job is to move that power across the country and deliver it at a voltage our equipment can actually use, around 230 V in a Swedish home, while holding the frequency steady at 50 Hz. Everything that follows is really about how it manages those two things at once - hold the voltage at the level it is supposed to be and steady frequency.

A power plant, say a hydropower station, generates electricity at a fairly high voltage, in the range of tens of kilovolts. But that voltage has to stepped down on the way to us. The ability to change voltage on demand is what makes our current grid. We use alternating current (AC) rather than direct current (DC) precisely because AC voltage can be raised or lowered easily, and with very little loss, using transformers. If you want to look up how AC won to run our grid, please google "war of the currents" between Edison and Tesla.

In fact, right off the production site, the voltage is stepped up to a much higher range - from tens of kV to hundreds of kV. This is done because of transmission losses. When current flows through a wire, some energy is always lost as heat, and those losses grow quickly with distance. Stepping up the voltage reduces the amount of current through the wire for the same part, and therefore far smaller losses over long distances. This step up in voltage is done by a step-up transformer in a substation. It marks the official start of the transmission grid.

Transmission lines run at very high voltage: in Sweden, 220 kV and 400 kV, while worldwide the range runs from roughly 155 kV up to 765 kV. These lines are strung between large steel towers, though they can also be buried underground. They are usually three conducting lines, one for each phase of the AC supply, and sometimes a thinner wire along the very top that acts as a ground wire to protect against lightning. AC lines become uneconomic beyond a few hundred kilometres, which is why the very longest links and undersea cables use high-voltage DC (HVDC) instead. Sweden has several, such as the Fenno-Skan link to Finland.

As the power approaches the area where it will be used, the whole process runs in reverse. The voltage is stepped down, again in a substation, this time with a step-down transformer. That substation also holds a distribution bus, which splits the power and sends it off in several directions, along with the circuit breakers and switches that can disconnect a line if something goes wrong. From here, lower-voltage distribution lines carry the electricity the final stretch into our houses.

In Sweden this sequence of voltages is handed down through a chain of operators. Svenska kraftnät (SvK), the transmission system operator, owns the national grid at 400 kV and 220 kV and is responsible for keeping the whole system at 50 Hz. It steps the voltage down to around 130 kV and passes the power to a regional network operator, such as Vattenfall Eldistribution, E.ON Energidistribution or Ellevio, which carries it onward and steps it down further, from 130 kV to roughly 10 to 20 kV. Finally, a local network operator delivers it to homes and businesses, at 400 V across the three phases, or 230 V for ordinary single-phase equipment.

The frequency in this whole sequence only stays steady if the system can absorb sudden shocks, such as a lightning strike, a snapped line, or the loss of a whole power station. This is where traditional plants quietly do a great deal of work. They generate with heavy turbines and shafts spinning at high speed, and that spinning mass stores energy. When something goes wrong, this stored energy gives the system inertia, its natural resistance to change, so the frequency decreases slowly instead of collapsing suddenly. It behaves rather like a child's spinning top, which resists being knocked over once it is going. Wind turbines and solar panels connect through power electronics and add little or no spinning inertia of their own, so as more renewables join the grid, operators increasingly rely on other tools, such as fast reserves that react within seconds, to do the same job.

In case of deviation,frequency regulation is done through four reserves or more commonly known as ancillary services. When load and production fall out of balance for any reason, SvK restores the grid through a sequence of frequency reserve products. The main three are FCR (Frequency Containment Reserve), aFRR (automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve), and mFRR (manual Frequency Restoration Reserve). In conditions of low inertia, when there is not enough spinning mass on the grid to cushion sudden changes, a fourth product, FFR (Fast Frequency Reserve), is also deployed.

0–2 s

FFR Fast Frequency Reserve

In conditions of low inertia, FFR is deployed as the first response. It is available only as upward capacity, that is, to inject power, and serves to prevent automatic load-shedding when frequency falls too quickly.

Upward only · Automatic
≤ 30 s

FCR Frequency Containment Reserve

FCR is the first line of defence in an imbalance. When frequency falls below or rises above the 49.9 / 50.1 Hz band, FCR activates automatically within 30 seconds, adjusting power output up or down and holding it for up to 15 minutes. FCR-N (normal) handles small bidirectional deviations around 50 Hz, while FCR-D (disturbance) handles larger one-directional deviations following a sudden event. FCR is fully decentralised and automatic: each participating asset measures its own frequency and acts without any signal from the TSO.

FCR-N bidirectional · FCR-D one-way · Automatic
≤ 5 min

aFRR automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve

aFRR is the next line of defence. It is dispatched automatically by the TSO within five minutes of an imbalance event and restores balance by adjusting either load or generation. It builds on the response initiated by FCR and continues to act until the frequency is fully restored to 50 Hz.

Bidirectional · TSO-dispatched
≤ 12.5 min

mFRR manual Frequency Restoration Reserve

mFRR is reserved for longer or larger imbalances. It is dispatched manually by the TSO, and either supports or replaces aFRR, and can also be activated preventively, before an imbalance fully develops. It must be active within 12.5 minutes of the TSO's signal, and must hold for at least five minutes.

Bidirectional · Manual

So what actually happens when a fault occurs? The sequence is always the same: detect, isolate, restore. Measurement devices and protection relays spread across the grid sense sudden changes in voltage, current and other key signals. Once a fault is confirmed, a digital relay sends a trip signal and a circuit breaker opens to cut the faulty section off, with backup protection standing by in case the main breaker fails to act. The whole point is to keep the disturbance local, because the grid is so tightly interconnected that an unchecked fault could ripple outward through the rest of it. Only then comes the repair, and how long that takes depends on the fault: an instrument upset by a lightning surge is a quick fix, while a physically damaged line can take much longer.

Overseeing the entire process is a control system called SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition). From the national control room it watches the whole transmission grid in real time, tracking frequency, voltage, line loading and switch positions, and sends commands back out to the equipment. That room runs 24 hours a day, every day of the year, making the continuous small adjustments that keep production and consumption in balance at 50 Hz.

Terminology

A handful of concepts and acronyms that recur throughout the site.

Operator

TSO (Transmission System Operator)

In Sweden, this is Svenska kraftnät, a fully state-owned authority. Its responsibility is to balance load and production in real time, and to maintain the frequency at 50 Hz. It also owns and maintains the high-voltage transmission backbone (220 kV and 400 kV). Because it is accountable for system balance, the TSO also procures and operates ancillary services.

Operator

DSO (Distribution System Operator)

Sweden has roughly 170 DSOs, of which three are regional: Vattenfall Eldistribution (state-owned, covering western, central and northern Sweden), Ellevio (private, Stockholm and central Sweden), and E.ON (private, southern Sweden). Regional DSOs deliver electricity to local DSOs and to high-consumption customers; local DSOs, for example Göteborg Energi or Lerum Energi, distribute it to end customers.

Concept

Ancillary services

The services required to maintain stable frequency and voltage on the grid. They include frequency regulation, voltage support (to manage reactive power), operating reserves such as spinning and non-spinning reserves used for demand spikes or generator failures, and black-start capability, which allows the grid to be restarted after a complete blackout. The section below elaborates on frequency regulation specifically, since it is the most active service from day to day.

Economics

LCOE (Levelised Cost of Energy)

The all-in cost of producing one unit of electricity over the full life of a power plant, including the upfront investment, fuel, maintenance, and decommissioning, divided by the total energy the plant will produce. Quoted in currency per MWh (for example, USD/MWh or EUR/MWh). LCOE is the standard way to compare different generation technologies on a like-for-like basis.

Economics

CapEx (Capital Expenditure)

The upfront money required to build a power plant. Includes the equipment (panels, turbines, reactor), the land, the construction work, and the grid connection. CapEx is paid once at the start. Solar, wind, hydro and nuclear are all CapEx-heavy. Gas plants are not.

Economics

OpEx (Operating Expenditure)

The ongoing cost of running the plant year after year. Includes fuel, maintenance, staff, insurance, and grid fees. Solar and wind have very low OpEx because the fuel (sunlight, wind) is free. Nuclear has moderate OpEx. Gas and coal have high OpEx because the fuel itself is expensive.

Deployment

Behind-the-meter

Generation or storage installed on the customer's side of the electricity meter. Rooftop solar, home batteries, and household EV chargers are all behind-the-meter. The owner consumes the output directly and only interacts with the grid for excess or shortfall.

Deployment

Front-of-the-meter

Generation or storage connected directly to the public grid, on the utility's side of the meter. Large solar parks, wind farms, nuclear plants and grid-scale batteries are all front-of-the-meter. Output is sold into the wholesale market (Nord Pool) or through long-term Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs).

Sweden's four bidding zones

The country is divided into four electricity price zones. Production sits mostly in the north, demand mostly in the south. When the wires between them are full, the southern zones clear at higher prices.

SE1 (Far north) Luleå region. Sparse population. Heavy hydro and wind production. Lowest prices.
SE2 (North) Sundsvall down to roughly Hudiksvall. Major hydro on the big rivers. Still low prices.
SE3 (Central) Stockholm, Göteborg, Uppsala. Most of the population. Moderate prices, moderate production.
SE4 (South) Malmö and surrounding Skåne. High demand, limited local production. Most expensive zone.

Workings of the Swedish market

From a turbine spinning in the north to a kettle boiling in the south. How the trade is organised, who the players are, and where the money flows.

A deregulated market with a regulated monopoly

Since the 1996 deregulation, the part of Swedish electricity you buy has operated as a competitive market: consumers choose their supplier. The part that delivers it to you, the local grid, remains a regulated monopoly determined by geography.

The distinction matters in practice. A supplier contract can be switched online in roughly five minutes. The grid operator cannot be changed at all: it is fixed by the address on the meter.

One Nordic market, four Swedish zones

Sweden trades on Nord Pool, the joint Nordic-Baltic power exchange. Wholesale prices are determined daily and hourly, denominated in euros per MWh, and set through a uniform marginal pricing model: in each hour, the most expensive accepted bid sets the clearing price paid to every accepted seller.

Within Sweden, the country is divided into four bidding zones (SE1–SE4) so the market reflects internal transmission bottlenecks rather than masking them. When the corridors from north to south are saturated, the southern zones clear at higher prices than the northern ones.

Why this matters: two households paying the same supplier may see very different bills in the same hour, simply because they live in different zones.

How a kilowatt-hour is traded

02

At 13:00 CET each day, Nord Pool matches supply and demand for the following day. The most expensive bid still required sets the clearing price: bids above it do not sell, bids at or below it are paid that price.

Svenska kraftnät manages real-time balance and operates an intraday market for last-minute adjustments, plus a balancing market for the very final hour. Three markets, in sequence.

The meter records consumption each hour. The supplier pays the spot price (or a contracted fixed average), and bills the consumer for it plus margin.

The one rule the whole thing exists to obey

Because nobody can predict the future perfectly, Sweden does not try to solve balancing load and production with one market. It uses a relay of markets, each one running closer and closer to real time, each one a chance to fix the mistakes of the one before. Picture a relay race where each runner cleans up a little more of the mess the last runner left.

Round one: the big daily auction (the day-ahead market)

This is the main event, and it is where the vast majority of Sweden's electricity is actually bought and sold. It runs on Nord Pool, the shared Nordic and Baltic power exchange.

Here is the simplified version. Every day, around noon, everyone who makes electricity and everyone who needs a lot of it writes down two things for each 15-minute block of tomorrow: how much they want to sell or buy, and the price at which they are willing to do it. Then an algorithm lines up all the sellers cheapest-first and all the buyers most-eager-first, and finds the single price where the two lines cross. Everyone who made the cut trades at that one price, even the cheap sellers.

Here is a bit more complicated version: capacities on the interconnectors are published at 10:00 CET, and buyers and sellers have until 12:00 CET to submit their final bids for delivery the next day. The orders are matched across the whole pan-European coupling through a shared algorithm called Euphemia, and the single clearing price for each block and each bidding zone is set where the sell curve and the buy curve meet, taking grid constraints into account. The prices are then announced to the market at about 12:45 CET.

Two features make this fair and a little surprising.

Marginal (uniform) pricing

The first is marginal (uniform) pricing, sometimes called pay-as-clear. The most expensive bid that still has to be accepted to meet demand sets the price that everyone receives. So if cheap hydro and nuclear clear the market but the last megawatt needed comes from an expensive gas plant, the hydro and nuclear get paid the gas price too. This sounds like a giveaway, but it is deliberate: it rewards the cheapest producers most and gives everyone an honest incentive to bid their true cost.

A quick example. Imagine four producers offer to sell power for the 18:00 to 18:15 block: hydro at 10 EUR/MWh, nuclear at 20, wind at 0 (the wind is free, so it bids low to make sure it sells), and a gas plant at 90. Demand that quarter-hour needs all but the gas plant. The clearing price is set by the most expensive accepted bid, the nuclear at 20. Everyone who got in, including the wind that offered 0, is paid 20 EUR/MWh. The gas plant sits idle because nobody needed power that expensive.

Four bidding zones

The second feature is that Sweden is not one price area but four bidding zones, SE1 to SE4, running from the far north down to Malmö. Most of the cheap generation (hydro and wind) sits in the north, and most of the demand sits in the south. The transmission wires between them can only carry so much. When those wires fill up, the auction is forced to set a higher price in the south than in the north, because southern buyers cannot reach the cheap northern power. This is why two households on the same supplier can pay very different amounts in the same quarter-hour simply because of where they live.

The recent change worth knowing. Until autumn 2025 these blocks were a full hour each. From the trading day of 30 September 2025, for delivery on 1 October 2025, the day-ahead market switched from 60-minute to 15-minute blocks across all European bidding zones. That means traders now manage 96 quarter-hour intervals per day instead of 24 hourly ones. The point of the change is that wind and solar jump around within the hour, and finer blocks let the market track those jumps and waste less. An hourly price is still published as the simple average of the four quarter-hour prices, for anyone who needs it.

Round two: the afternoon and overnight fix-ups (the intraday market)

The day-ahead auction is a plan made the day before, and plans go wrong. A wind forecast turns out too optimistic, a power plant trips offline, a cold snap arrives early. The intraday market, also run by Nord Pool, is where participants trade among themselves to patch these gaps after the day-ahead result is known and right up until close to real time.

The simple version: it is the marketplace that stays open after the big auction has closed. If you are a supplier who sold more than you can now cover, you log on and buy the missing power from someone who finds they have a surplus.

Mechanically there are two flavours running side by side. There is continuous trading, where deals happen one-to-one the moment a buyer and a seller agree, all day, every day. And there are intraday auctions layered on top. After the day-ahead price is settled, an intraday auction opens at 15:00 for the same next-day period, another runs at 22:00, and a third at 10:00 covering the remaining hours of the same day. These also now run in 15-minute resolution.

Round three onward: the last-second stabilisers (the balancing markets)

Now we hand over from Nord Pool to Svenska kraftnät, Sweden's state-owned transmission system operator. From a few minutes before real time and into the second-by-second present, Svenska kraftnät is the only body responsible for keeping the system in balance. It does not make a profit on this; it is a public authority whose job is system security.

To do it, Svenska kraftnät buys a set of standby services called ancillary services or reserves. The faster the helper has to react, the more specialised it is. There are five of them, ordered from fastest to slowest.

FFR (Fast Frequency Reserve) is the sprinter, used in summer when the grid is light and wobbles easily. It is procured on a yearly basis and cleared twice a week, on Mondays and Fridays.

FCR-N (Frequency Containment Reserve, Normal) handles the constant small wobbles around 50 Hz. It gives a symmetrical response, pushing up or down as the frequency drifts between about 49.9 and 50.1 Hz.

FCR-D (Frequency Containment Reserve, Disturbance) is the emergency catch for big shocks, like a nuclear unit tripping. It has separate up and down products and activates on larger deviations, beyond roughly 49.5 or 50.5 Hz. Svenska kraftnät and Energinet buy both FCR-N and FCR-D from a shared Danish-Swedish market with two auctions, one before day-ahead closes and one after, priced at the margin.

aFRR (automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve) is the automatic nudge that restores the frequency back to exactly 50 Hz after FCR has caught a wobble. It is activated centrally for the whole Nordic area from a single controller, which calculates how much is needed and shares the request out to each TSO pro-rata. This one has just been expanded: from 3 March 2025, aFRR began being procured for all hours as part of the move to the new automated Nordic balancing model.

mFRR (manual Frequency Restoration Reserve) is the slower, larger reserve that frees the faster ones back up so they are ready for the next event. It is bought both on a capacity market a day ahead and on an energy market cleared in real time, with energy bids submitted no later than 45 minutes before the operating quarter-hour.

Who is allowed to provide these? Not just anyone with a power plant. To supply FCR-N, FCR-D, aFRR or mFRR you must be, become, or work through a Balancing Service Provider (BSP), which is Svenska kraftnät's legal counterpart for delivery. A hydro plant, a big battery, an aggregated fleet of home batteries, even a paper mill willing to dim its load can all become reserve providers through a BSP.

This is the layer where Sweden's edge-of-the-system companies live, because batteries and bundled small devices are excellent at the fast products. The recurring Swedish players here are aggregators and virtual power plants such as CheckWatt, Flower, Sympower and Ingrid Capacity, alongside the big hydro owners. To give a sense of the money involved, Svenska kraftnät's own 2024 to 2025 monthly reports put FCR-D in the region of 5 to 7 EUR/MW/h, aFRR around 16 to 20 EUR/MW/h, and FCR-N around 25 to 30 EUR/MW/h. For an old hydro plant whose dam was paid off decades ago, this reserve income is now a serious part of the business.

The scorekeeper at the end: who pays when the plan was wrong

After every quarter-hour has actually happened, somebody has to add up who promised what, who delivered what, and who therefore owes money for the imbalance Svenska kraftnät had to fix. This is imbalance settlement, and it runs on a simple principle of fairness: the polluter pays.

The key character here is the Balance Responsible Party (BRP). Every unit of production and consumption in Sweden must sit under a BRP, who promises to keep its own little corner balanced. In practice there are always deviations from the plan, and these deviations are called imbalances. When a BRP fails to balance, Svenska kraftnät steps in by buying or selling the missing or surplus electricity, and the party that caused the imbalance has to pay the cost of putting it right.

The arithmetic is not done by Svenska kraftnät directly but by eSett Oy, a Finnish company. eSett is owned in equal shares by the four Nordic TSOs (Energinet, Fingrid, Statnett and Svenska kraftnät) and performs the common imbalance settlement and invoicing for all of them. The benefit of one shared settler is that a BRP has a single interface and one rulebook for the whole Nordic market. Settlement now runs in 15-minute blocks too: eSett moved to 15-minute imbalance settlement on 22 May 2023.

A worked example. A wind farm's BRP sells 100 MWh in the day-ahead auction for the 14:00 to 14:15 block, expecting a steady breeze. The wind dies and the farm delivers only 80 MWh. Svenska kraftnät had to make up the missing 20 MWh from its reserves in real time. eSett calculates the imbalance, and the wind farm's BRP is charged for that 20 MWh at the imbalance price, which can be a good deal more painful than the day-ahead price it earned. This is exactly why forecasting and the intraday market matter so much: every megawatt you can correct in advance is a megawatt you do not get penalised for later.

The backstop behind everything: Effektreserven

For the genuinely worst moments, typically a cold, still, dark winter morning when even the markets cannot clear, Svenska kraftnät keeps a separate Effektreserven, a strategic reserve of contracted standby capacity and demand reduction that it can call on. It sits outside the normal day-ahead trade and activates only when the ordinary mechanisms run out of road. Think of it as the spare generator in the basement that you hope never to switch on.

The full cast of players

It helps to see everyone in one place, because the same names recur in different roles.

Generation

Producers

Make the electricity and bid it in. The big ones are Vattenfall (state-owned, the largest), Fortum (Finnish), Statkraft (Norwegian state-owned) and Uniper, plus industrial and renewable players like Holmen and OX2 and municipal utilities such as Skellefteå Kraft and Jämtkraft. Some sell into Nord Pool; some sign long-term PPAs straight to industrial buyers.

Exchange (NEMO)

Nord Pool

Owned by the regional grid operators, it runs the day-ahead and intraday auctions. It is not alone: EPEX SPOT is also a licensed Nominated Electricity Market Operator in the Nordics, and matching is governed by the European frameworks SDAC (day-ahead) and SIDC (intraday) using the Euphemia algorithm.

TSO

Svenska kraftnät

Owns the 220 and 400 kV backbone, keeps the frequency at 50 Hz, and runs the balancing markets. Its Nordic siblings are Statnett (Norway), Fingrid (Finland) and Energinet (Denmark), and much of the balancing is now coordinated across all four.

Settlement

eSett Oy

Jointly owned by the four Nordic TSOs, it calculates and invoices every imbalance, giving each BRP a single interface and one rulebook for the whole Nordic market.

Financial balance

Balance Responsible Parties

Carry the financial promise to keep production and consumption matched. The role gives access to the day-ahead, intraday and balancing markets at once, and the BRP is responsible for the gap between planned and actual production, consumption and trade.

Reserve delivery

Balancing Service Providers

The parties Svenska kraftnät contracts to actually deliver the reserves. A provider often needs both a BSP agreement and a BRP relationship at the delivery point.

Flexibility

Aggregators and VPPs

Bundle thousands of small devices (home batteries, EV chargers, industrial loads) so they can act as a single BSP-sized resource. Swedish examples: CheckWatt, Flower, Sympower, Ingrid Capacity, and consumer-facing players like Tibber that flex household demand.

Distribution

DSOs (nätbolag)

Own the local wires that bring power to your building and report the metering data everything else depends on. Sweden has roughly 170, the three big regionals being Vattenfall Eldistribution, Ellevio and E.ON. They are regulated monopolies, fixed by your address.

Retail

Suppliers (elhandelsbolag)

The brand on your contract. They buy on Nord Pool on your behalf and resell with a margin. Around 120 are active in Sweden, and you can switch in minutes.

Regulator

Energimarknadsinspektionen (Ei)

Approves grid tariffs and supervises conduct, coordinating with the EU body ACER and the European TSO network ENTSO-E. On the policy and consumer side sit Energimyndigheten and Konsumentverket.

Demand

You, the consumer

Sit at the very end. Your meter records what you use each quarter-hour, your supplier pays the zone's clearing price (or a contracted average), and bills you for it plus margin and grid fee.

Following a single quarter-hour all the way through

To tie it together, trace the 08:00 to 08:15 block on a winter Tuesday. The day before, around noon, producers and large buyers post sealed bids for that block into Nord Pool. Euphemia clears it at, say, 95 EUR/MWh in SE4 (high, because it is a cold morning and the wires from the north are full) and 40 EUR/MWh in SE1. Through the afternoon and night, a few plants trip and a forecast shifts, so participants trade on the intraday market to patch their positions. In the final 45 minutes, Svenska kraftnät lines up its mFRR energy bids; in the final minutes and seconds, FCR and aFRR catch and correct the live wobbles automatically. The block happens. Afterwards, eSett tots up who delivered what, and any BRP whose actual differed from its plan is charged or paid for the difference. If the whole system had still fallen short, Effektreserven would have been called. The system stayed level, and the money flowed to match.

As a game analogy

The Swedish system is best understood not as one market but as a relay of them, handing off from a day before real time down to the live second, each round priced and settled separately, all in service of one physical rule: supply must equal demand at 50 Hz, always. Nord Pool runs the planning rounds, Svenska kraftnät runs the real-time rounds, eSett keeps the score, and a polluter-pays settlement makes sure the people who caused imbalances are the ones who fund the fix. The 2025 shift to 15-minute blocks across day-ahead, intraday and settlement is the single biggest recent change, and it pushes the whole machine to track the fast swings of wind and solar more honestly than the old hourly system ever could.

A closer look: what am I actually paying for?

The same bill, unpacked: who charges you, why the two halves behave so differently, and why the grid fee keeps rising.

The price you get at the end of the month comes from two entities, your electricity supplier and your DSO. Sometimes they are the same company: in Gothenburg, for example, Göteborg Energi can be both your supplier and your DSO. The supplier charges you for the actual energy you consumed that month, and you can choose between competing suppliers, each one incentivised to buy electricity as cheaply as possible. The DSO is the company that physically delivers electricity to your house. It is fixed by the area you live in, with no option to switch, and it charges you for transporting the electricity through its wires rather than for the energy itself.

Part 1: the energy itself (the supplier, the "buy cheap" part)

The electricity supplier (elhandelsföretag) is the one playing the wholesale market. Almost all Nordic power is traded on the Nord Pool exchange in a day-ahead auction, which sets an hourly price (now quarter-hourly since 2025) for each bidding zone. Sweden has four zones, SE1 in the far north down to SE4 in the south, and because most generation sits in the hydro-rich north while most demand is in the south, the southern zones are usually pricier and more volatile (see the market section for more detail).

The supplier buys at this wholesale (spot) price and resells it to you. How that reaches you depends on your contract:

  • Spot or hourly contract (rörligt / timpris): the supplier passes the wholesale price straight through and adds a small markup. You get the cheap hours when they happen, but you also carry the risk of the expensive ones. This is the "buy cheap" model in its purest form, and it is what the smart-home apps (Tibber, Greenely) are built to exploit by shifting your use to cheap hours.
  • Fixed contract (fast pris): the supplier locks a price for you and protects itself by buying forward on the futures market. You pay a premium for that certainty.

The supplier's own profit is a markup (påslag, a few öre per kWh) plus a small monthly fee. The wholesale spot price itself is roughly 30 to 40% of the total bill. The key point: a competitive supplier has every incentive to buy as cheaply as it can, because that is what it competes on. This is the part of your bill that moves with the market.

Part 2: the network fee (the DSO, the "delivery" part)

The DSO owns the poles, wires, transformers and meters between the national grid and your fuse box. It does not produce or trade energy. Its fee (nätavgift) pays to build, run, maintain and renew that local network, to handle outages, and to pass through the charges it itself pays to the regional grid and to the national TSO, Svenska kraftnät, plus the cost of the electricity it must buy to cover physical losses in the wires.

The network fee normally has two or three parts:

  • A fixed fee, set by the size of your main fuse or subscribed capacity. A bigger connection costs more because it reserves more of the network's capacity for you.
  • A transfer fee (elöverföring) in öre per kWh, for the volume you actually move through the wires.
  • Increasingly, a power or capacity charge (effekttariff) based on your highest peak demand in kW, not just total energy. Now that smart meters read consumption hourly or every 15 minutes, DSOs can bill you for how hard you hit the network at its busiest, which is closer to what actually drives their costs. This is why charging your EV slowly overnight can be cheaper than blasting it at full power during the evening peak.

Crucially, unlike the supplier, the DSO is a monopoly. There is only one grid operator in each geographical area, and the consumer cannot switch, because it would be far too expensive to build parallel grids. That is exactly why this part of the bill is regulated.

Part 3: taxes on top

On top of both parts the state adds an energy tax (energiskatt) per kWh, lower in certain northern municipalities, and then 25% VAT on everything, including on the tax itself. So your true total is: energy (supplier) plus network fee (DSO) plus energy tax plus VAT.

How much the DSO is allowed to charge: the revenue cap

Because the DSO is a monopoly, it cannot simply set whatever price it likes. Sweden's regulator, the Energy Markets Inspectorate (Energimarknadsinspektionen, "Ei"), sets an ex-ante revenue cap (intäktsram) for each of the roughly 170 DSOs, fixed in advance for four-year periods (the current one runs 2024 to 2027). The DSO's total income from all its customers may not exceed that cap. The cap is built from a few ingredients:

  • A return on the capital base. The DSO is allowed to earn a regulated rate of return (a WACC, the kalkylränta, in the region of a few per cent) on the depreciated value of all the assets it has in the ground, its regulatory asset base.
  • Depreciation of those assets over their long lives (current-carrying components are depreciated over around 40 years).
  • Operating costs to run and maintain the network.
  • Quality and efficiency adjustments. Ei runs an incentive scheme that rewards reliability and penalises poor performance, so a DSO with many long outages effectively earns less, while one that keeps the lights on and runs efficiently is allowed a bit more.

This is the mechanism that decides your network fee, and it is the direct reason the fee rises when the DSO invests.

Why the fee goes up when the DSO does network work (the positive read)

The chain of logic is simple. The DSO's allowed revenue is largely a return on the value of the assets it owns. When it builds new lines, upgrades substations or replaces old cables, the value of its asset base goes up. A bigger asset base means a higher allowed return plus more depreciation to recover, which means a higher revenue cap, which flows through to a higher network fee. When Ei raised the revenue caps for grid companies, grid fees rose accordingly, which is exactly what Swedish households saw in 2025.

It is easy to read that as "they charge me more for the same thing." It is more accurate, and fairer, to read it as paying for a genuine upgrade to shared infrastructure that you and everyone after you will use. Concretely, that extra charge is buying:

  • Capacity for electrification. Heat pumps, EV charging and the electrification of industry and transport are pushing far more power through local grids than they were built for. Without reinforcement, new connections get refused and the queue grows. The investment is what lets your neighbourhood actually plug in an EV charger or a heat pump without tripping the local transformer.
  • Connecting renewables. Rooftop solar, wind and batteries all need a grid that can absorb two-way flows. The upgrade is what makes it possible to connect them and to export your own solar.
  • Replacing an ageing grid. Much of the Swedish network was built decades ago and is reaching the end of its life. Replacing it now, in a planned way, is cheaper and less disruptive than waiting for it to fail.
  • Reliability. Because Ei's framework financially rewards fewer and shorter outages, money spent on weatherproofing, burying vulnerable lines and adding automation directly buys a more dependable supply, especially through Swedish winters.
  • Building ahead of demand. A well-run DSO invests before the bottleneck bites, so capacity is there when the new factory, data centre or housing district arrives. Researchers note that these network expansions genuinely benefit society, even though they raise the revenue the DSO needs to recover.

And the reassuring part is that this is not a blank cheque. Because an independent regulator caps the total, checks that the costs are reasonable, sets the allowed return, and links part of it to quality, the increase you see is, by design, tied to real, audited investment in a grid that has to carry a far more electrified Sweden. You are essentially pre-paying, through a regulated and supervised mechanism, for the headroom that the energy transition needs.

The one-line version. Your supplier competes to buy energy cheaply on Nord Pool and sells it to you with a small markup; your DSO is a regulated local monopoly that charges a separate, capped fee to deliver it; and when that fee rises because of network work, you are paying for more capacity, more reliability and a future-proofed grid, with a regulator making sure the increase reflects real investment rather than monopoly profit.
